What a split test actually measures

A split test compares two or more variants of the same thing, like a headline, a button color, or an onboarding flow, by showing each variant to a separate, randomly assigned group of users and comparing outcomes. The point isn't to prove a variant is "better" in the abstract; it's to estimate the effect of one specific change on one specific metric, under the traffic and audience you actually have right now.

Before you start

  • Write down the metric you're optimizing for, in advance, not after glancing at results.
  • Pick a minimum sample size or run duration up front, and stick to it.
  • Make sure assignment is random and sticky (a user shouldn't flip variants mid-session).
  • Only change one variable per test, or you can't attribute the effect.

Common pitfalls

  • Peeking: checking results daily and stopping the moment one variant looks ahead inflates false positives.
  • Underpowered tests: small sample sizes produce noisy results that look like signal.
  • Novelty effects: a new variant can outperform briefly just because it's new, then regress.
  • Metric mismatch: optimizing clicks when the real goal is retention or revenue.

Reading the result

A statistically significant result tells you the observed difference is unlikely to be pure chance. It does not tell you the effect will hold forever, generalize to a different audience, or matter enough to be worth shipping. Treat every test result as a local estimate, not a universal truth, and re-test meaningful changes after the audience or product shifts.
